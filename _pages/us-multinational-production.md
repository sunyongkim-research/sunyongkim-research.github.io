### I. US Multinational Production and the Global Financial Cycle  [[SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5580571)] 
<span style="color:red; font-style:italic;">Revise and Resubmit (AEJ Macro)</span>

<img src="/assets/MNC_Diff_Plots.png" alt="Pax-Americana Chart" width="75%">

**Abstract:**
This paper links the global financial cycle to the economic geography of U.S. multinational (MNC) presence.   I build a multi-country general equilibrium model in which large U.S. MNC transmit firm specific shocks internationally through their foreign affiliates. These shocks aggregate into a granular global factor that drives correlations in consumption, interest rates, credit spreads, and risky asset returns. Countries more exposed to U.S. MNC presence also display stronger synchronization in equities, bonds, and exchange rates. To quantify their role in driving global risk premia, I conduct a structural estimation model. This exercise generates a highly granular world economy that reproduces average equity premia of 2–4%, real short rates near 1%, and cross country asset correlations of 0.8–0.9, broadly in line with post-1980 data. A policy counterfactual inspired by recent U.S. antitrust proposals shows that reducing firm concentration by 25–50% lowers global risk premia by roughly 15–30%, compresses volatility of premia by 40%, and reduces cross-country financial synchronization by 6–10 percentage points, generating welfare gains of 5–30 basis points per year—largest in highly exposed economies. The results highlight how industrial concentration endogenously shapes both the origin and amplitude of global financial cycles.
